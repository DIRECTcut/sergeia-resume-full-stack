# Offer In A Month

## Mindset

- Treat foreign remote search like a repeatable process, not a mystery.
- Do not wait for perfect English, perfect prep, or secret channels.
- Judge progress by applications, replies, and interviews, not by mood.
- Expect the first solid result to take weeks, not days.

## English

- Do not block the search on English perfection.
- Apply even if your English is only good enough for basic work communication.
- Optimize for being understandable, not polished.
- Practice explaining your work, asking clarifying questions, and answering follow-ups.

## Target

- Sell your current stack first before planning a full retrain.
- Start with markets and companies where your experience is already usable.
- Consider foreign companies with Russian-speaking teams as a valid entry point.
- Aim for roles where you are already strong enough to interview now.

## Resume

- Build a clear resume fast and improve it while applying.
- Keep it simple, readable, and keyword-friendly.
- Show impact, not task lists.
- Use numbers and outcomes wherever possible.
- Do not overinvest in endless ATS tweaking before getting real market feedback.

## LinkedIn

- Fill the profile completely.
- Use a location that matches the market you want to target.
- Make your headline and summary searchable by role and stack.
- Treat LinkedIn as a daily tool, not a one-time setup task.

## Search

- Start applying before everything feels perfect.
- Use LinkedIn as the default main channel.
- Apply every day as routine.
- Treat 5 applications per day as a floor, not a ceiling.
- Increase volume while staying below obvious spam behavior.
- Do not escape into obscure job boards just because LinkedIn feels hard.

## Outreach

- Apply first, then message recruiters or relevant team members when possible.
- Keep messages short and specific.
- Prioritize channels that produce replies over channels that only feel clever.
- Ignore “secret method” thinking until the basics are fully done.

## Interviews

- Prepare self-introduction before the first interview invite arrives.
- Sell yourself directly; do not speak like you are apologizing for being there.
- Answer `Tell me about yourself` with a concise narrative: who you are, what you do well, what results you create, and why this role fits.
- Include a little personality and hobbies so you sound like a future colleague, not only a skill list.
- Mirror the interviewer’s level of personal openness and communication style.

## Tactics

- Ignore algorithm prep unless real interviews show it is required.
- Do not spend time solving legal/tax/relocation setup before you have an offer.
- Solve paperwork only after a concrete offer appears.
- Use the notice period and hiring lead time to handle setup later.

## Routine

- Every day: open LinkedIn, apply, and maintain momentum.
- Every day: improve one weak point in resume, profile, or outreach.
- Every week: review which applications got replies and double down on that pattern.
- Keep going for at least 1-3 months before judging the process.

## Traps

- Waiting for C1 English.
- Overstudying instead of applying.
- Obsessing over algorithms too early.
- Looking for secret recruiter chats instead of fixing the basics.
- Spending hours on incorporation, banking, or tax setup before any offer exists.
- Expecting immediate results after a few applications.
