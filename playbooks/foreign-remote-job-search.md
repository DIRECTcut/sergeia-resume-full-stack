# Foreign Remote Job Search

## Mindset

- Treat the search like a pipeline, not a lucky break.
- Expect the search to take weeks or months, not days.
- Optimize for interview volume and offer quality, not emotional attachment to any one vacancy.
- Keep a tracker for roles, contacts, stages, feedback, and follow-ups.

## Target

- Aim for mid-to-senior roles; junior hiring is much rarer.
- Default to roles where your existing stack is already marketable.
- Use English even if the channel is local, unless the role is clearly local-only.
- Judge compensation by hourly return and working conditions, not monthly number alone.

## Resume

- Keep it plain, one page first, PDF only.
- Put the most important facts at the top: role, years, timezone, contact, LinkedIn, GitHub.
- Write for ATS first, recruiter second, technical reviewer third.
- Remove anything that adds friction: age, Russia mention, salary demands, remote-only demands.
- Use a clear `About` section that says what you do, where you add value, and what environments you have handled.
- In experience, use achievement bullets, not duty bullets.
- Every bullet should show: what you did, why it mattered, what changed.
- Use numbers wherever possible, even approximate ones, to make impact legible.
- Expand only the last 3 roles in detail; compress older roles.
- Keep each prior role at 1+ year on paper where possible to avoid looking unstable.
- Add a broad but relevant `Skills` block for ATS keyword coverage.
- Include GitHub and make it non-empty; a profile README is enough if needed.

## Cover Letter

- Keep one reusable master version.
- Use it to confirm fit, motivation, and relevance.
- Customize only for unusually strong target companies.
- Do not spend hours per application on cover letters.

## LinkedIn

- Fill everything.
- Add a strong banner with role, years, stack, and 1-2 proof points.
- Use a professional photo.
- Turn on `Open to Work`.
- Make the headline keyword-dense: role variants, years, core stack.
- Make the first 4 lines of `About` stand on their own.
- Add top skills and also repeat important keywords in text.
- Use clickable links and a clean custom URL.
- Make the last roles fully filled: company, bullets, skills, media.
- Add up to 50 skills and order them by importance.
- Add certifications, featured links, recommendations, interests, and services where relevant.

### Content

- Do not leave LinkedIn activity empty.
- Post 2-3 times per week.
- Mix technical notes, reposts with commentary, small lessons, and light personal content.
- Use a small trusted circle to like/comment early and boost initial engagement.
- Reply to comments quickly.

## Search

- Use several channels in parallel.
- Use LinkedIn passive inbound.
- Use LinkedIn job applications.
- Use direct outreach to recruiters.
- Use direct outreach to hiring managers or team leads.
- Use HeadHunter.
- Use niche chats, communities, and referrals.
- Prioritize proven channels over obscure aggregators.
- Avoid investing much time in no-name job boards unless they clearly show live activity and relevant roles.
- Batch applications on a schedule instead of applying randomly.

## Outreach

- Save reusable message templates for recruiter replies, follow-ups, and interest messages.
- When a job is relevant, message the recruiter directly if possible.
- If needed, message team leads or employees and ask for referral or routing.
- Be brief, human, and specific.
- Keep follow-up discipline; if no answer after several days, ping again.

## Interviews

- Prepare before the first invite arrives.
- Practice technical answers in English, not just in your native language.
- Treat English as a communication threshold, not an exam in perfect grammar.
- Do many interviews; interview skill decays if you do not use it.
- Start with lower-priority companies to warm up.
- Record interviews when possible for review.
- Prepare `About me`.
- Prepare your reason for leaving.
- Prepare your target company profile.
- Prepare your compensation expectation.
- Prepare technical fundamentals.
- Prepare easy coding tasks.
- Prepare STAR stories for teamwork, conflict, failure, deadlines, and incidents.

## Interview Tactics

- On HR screens, be clear, upbeat, and easy to talk to.
- Never trash previous employers.
- On technical rounds, answer, then expand with real examples from your work.
- If you do not know something, reason out loud instead of freezing.
- Ask a few relevant questions every round.
- After each round, log weak spots and close them before the next one.
- Follow up if feedback stalls.

## Negotiation

- Always ask about compensation range.
- Always negotiate.
- Negotiate in writing where possible.
- Use competing processes or strong interview performance as leverage.
- Ask for a move upward even if the first offer feels acceptable.
- Treat bonuses, options, tokens, and future raises as uncertain until realized.

## Money

- Treat contracts as operational, not protective.
- Assume a contract role can end quickly and keep a buffer.
- Prefer payment paths you can document.
- Keep every invoice, contract, statement, and supporting file.
- If income handling is cross-border, verify the exact setup with a tax specialist for your jurisdiction.
- Optimize for something stable, repeatable, and explainable, not clever.

## Routine

- Weekly: update resume and LinkedIn if needed.
- Weekly: apply in batches.
- Weekly: do direct outreach.
- Weekly: publish a few LinkedIn posts.
- Weekly: practice technical and behavioral answers.
- Weekly: review interview notes.
- Monthly: take at least 1-2 interviews even if you are employed.
- Monthly: recalibrate your market rate from real conversations.
