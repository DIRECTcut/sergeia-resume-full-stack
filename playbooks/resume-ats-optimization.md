# Resume ATS Optimization

## Goal

- Optimize the resume so an ATS parses it correctly and a recruiter can filter for it quickly.
- Treat ATS as the first gate, not the final judge.
- Write for correct extraction first, recruiter scan second.

## What ATS Extracts

- Name
- Location
- Phone
- Email
- LinkedIn URL
- Job titles
- Companies
- Dates
- Education level
- Certifications
- Skills and keywords
- Implied years of experience per skill

If the parser reads these fields incorrectly, your resume can disappear from recruiter search even when your real experience is strong.

## Core Rules

- Use a plain, text-friendly layout. Avoid fancy templates, columns, graphics, and decorative formatting.
- Put the most important facts high on the page.
- Use standard section names such as `Professional Summary`, `Experience`, `Education`, `Skills`, `Certifications`.
- Use reverse chronological order.
- Include month and year for each role.
- Use recognizable job titles. If the internal title was obscure, normalize it to a market title recruiters will search for.
- Save as PDF only if the exported text remains selectable and parses cleanly. If not, use a simpler export path.

## Header

- First line: full name.
- Second line: exact location, contact details, and LinkedIn URL.
- Use a plain LinkedIn URL string, not hidden text behind a hyperlink.
- Include city, state or region, and country when targeting markets where recruiter filters depend on location.

## Remove Friction

- Remove photo.
- Remove date of birth.
- Remove marital status.
- Remove anything else unrelated to hiring fit that can create screening friction.

## Summary

- Add a short `Professional Summary`.
- State target role, years of experience, domain context, and a few high-value keywords.
- Include hard signals a recruiter can scan fast: product area, market, seniority, company types, metrics, or core stack.
- Keep it compact enough to read in seconds.

## Experience

- Format each role consistently: `Title`, `Company`, `Location`, `Start - End`.
- Add one short line for lesser-known companies or products so the recruiter immediately understands the context.
- If the company is not widely known, explain what the product or business does.
- Keep bullets aligned with the target role, not with every side task you ever touched.
- Use wording that reinforces the keywords you want ATS to attach to you.
- Avoid overemphasizing adjacent work if it can misclassify you.

Example:

- If you are targeting `Product Manager`, do not let a recent role over-index on `content marketing` wording unless that is genuinely the role you want next.

## Education

- List the highest degree first.
- Make the degree level easy to parse.
- Do not rely on the reader to infer whether `Master's` or `Bachelor's` is the highest level.

## Certifications

- Include relevant certifications, licenses, and substantial courses.
- Keep the names explicit and standardized so the parser can map them.

## Keywords

- ATS search is driven heavily by keywords.
- Recruiters often filter large pools by skill terms, domain terms, titles, and years of experience.
- The same keyword repeated naturally across summary, skills, and experience is stronger than a single isolated mention.
- Use the language the market uses, not only the language your current company uses.

## How To Find Keywords

- Collect around `30` target job descriptions for the same role.
- Mark the terms that repeat across them.
- Separate them into buckets: role titles, core skills, domain terms, tools and technologies, required methods or responsibilities.
- Rewrite your resume so the repeated terms appear where they are true.

The repeated terms are the closest thing to the recruiter search query.

## How To Apply Keywords

- Put the highest-value terms in the summary.
- Reflect them again in `Skills`.
- Reuse them in experience bullets where they are backed by real work.
- Prefer exact market wording when possible.
- Do not stuff keywords into invisible text, metadata, or nonsense lists.
- Do not claim keywords that are not supported by your experience.

## Per-Role Tailoring

- Do not assume one resume fits every application.
- Tailor the resume to each target role family.
- The goal is not a `100%` match to the job description. The practical threshold from the transcript is to look clearly relevant on at least `70-80%` of the stated requirements.
- Keep a stable base resume, then adapt keywords and emphasis for each role cluster.

## Parse Risks

- ATS is imperfect and can misread your profile.
- A misleading word in a recent role can cause the system to attach the wrong specialization to you.
- Old experience can inflate years of experience in the wrong skill.
- Rare internal titles can hide your relevance.

Because of this, test the resume as a parser would read it, not only as a human would read it.

## Self-Check

- Check that the header fields are extracted correctly.
- Check that location is parsed correctly.
- Check that each title is parsed correctly.
- Check that dates and tenure look right.
- Check that the highest education level is recognized.
- Check which skills the parser assigns to you.
- Check whether the most important target keywords are present enough times.
- Check whether any wrong specialization is being inferred from recent wording.

## Signals From Outcomes

- Immediate rejection can mean the resume is mismatched or poorly parsed.
- No instant rejection but no recruiter contact can mean the core keywords exist, but the resume is weaker than competing profiles in the same search.

This is not a perfect rule, but it is a useful diagnostic heuristic.

## Workflow

- Build one clean base resume.
- Choose one target role family.
- Review `30` relevant job descriptions.
- Extract repeated keywords.
- Rewrite summary, skills, and recent experience around those terms.
- Reduce wording that pushes you into the wrong category.
- Test how the resume parses.
- Fix misclassifications.
- Repeat for the next role family.

## Traps

- Fancy design that breaks extraction.
- Missing exact location.
- Hidden LinkedIn hyperlink instead of a visible URL.
- Obscure internal job titles.
- Missing month and year on roles.
- Weak or generic summary.
- Resume text that describes adjacent work better than the target work.
- Assuming ATS understands nuance.
- Keyword stuffing instead of truthful alignment.
