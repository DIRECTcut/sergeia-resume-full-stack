# Main Job Search Playbook

## Purpose

- Use this as the default playbook for finding a foreign remote role.
- Treat the search as a system: prepare the base assets, run applications consistently, improve from feedback, and negotiate from real process leverage.

## Core Principles

- Treat the search like a pipeline, not a lucky break.
- Measure progress by applications, replies, interviews, and offers, not by mood.
- Start before everything feels perfect.
- Expect traction in weeks or months, not days.
- Optimize for interview volume and offer quality, not emotional attachment to any one vacancy.
- Keep a tracker for roles, contacts, stages, feedback, and follow-ups.

## Targeting

- Aim for roles where your current stack is already marketable.
- Prefer mid-to-senior roles if your experience supports them; junior foreign remote hiring is rarer.
- Sell current strengths first before planning a major retrain.
- Use English by default unless the role is clearly local-only.
- Consider foreign companies with Russian-speaking teams as a valid entry point.
- Judge roles by effective compensation, working conditions, and stability, not just the headline monthly number.

## English

- Do not block the search on perfect English.
- Optimize for being understandable, not polished.
- Practice explaining your work, asking clarifying questions, and answering follow-ups in English.
- Treat English as a communication threshold, not an exam.

## Resume

- Build a clear resume quickly, then improve it while applying.
- Keep it plain, readable, one page first, and PDF only.
- Put the most important facts at the top: role, years, timezone, contact, LinkedIn, GitHub.
- Write for ATS first, recruiter second, technical reviewer third.
- Show achievements and outcomes, not duty lists.
- Make each bullet answer: what you did, why it mattered, and what changed.
- Use numbers wherever possible, even approximate ones.
- Expand the last 3 roles in detail and compress older ones.
- Keep prior roles stable on paper where possible to avoid unnecessary friction.
- Add a broad but relevant `Skills` section for keyword coverage.
- Remove friction from the document: no age, no Russia mention, no salary demands, no remote-only demands.
- Include GitHub and keep it non-empty; a solid profile README is enough if needed.

## Cover Letter

- Every application must contain a cover letter if possible.
- Keep one reusable master version.
- Customize only when a company is unusually strong or strategic.
- Do not spend large amounts of time per application on cover letters.

## LinkedIn

- Target a high SSI score (60+), see `playbooks/linkedin-SSI.md`.
- Fill the profile completely.
- Use a professional photo and a strong banner.
- Turn on `Open to Work`.
- Make the headline searchable by role variants, years, and core stack.
- Make the first lines of `About` stand on their own.
- Use a market-appropriate location for the roles you target.
- Fill recent roles with concrete bullets, skills, and links where relevant.
- Add top skills and repeat important keywords naturally in text.
- Use a clean custom URL and clickable links.
- Treat LinkedIn as a daily operating tool, not a one-time setup task.

## LinkedIn Content

- Do not leave activity empty.
- Post 2-3 times per week.
- Mix technical notes, reposts with commentary, small lessons, and light personal content.
- Reply to comments quickly.
- If available, use a small trusted circle for early engagement.

## Search Channels

- Use several channels in parallel.
- Use LinkedIn as the default main channel.
- Use LinkedIn passive inbound.
- Use LinkedIn job applications.
- Use direct outreach to recruiters.
- Use direct outreach to hiring managers, team leads, or relevant employees.
- Use HeadHunter.
- Use niche communities, chats, and referrals.
- Prioritize proven channels over obscure boards and “secret methods.”
- Avoid no-name job boards unless they clearly show live relevant activity.

## Application Strategy

- Start applying before the profile and resume feel perfect.
- Apply every day as routine.
- Treat 5 applications per day as a floor, not a ceiling.
- Increase volume while staying below obvious spam behavior.
- Batch applications and outreach on a schedule instead of acting randomly.
- Review which applications got replies and double down on that pattern.

## Outreach

- Apply first, then message recruiters or relevant team members when possible.
- Save reusable templates for recruiter replies, follow-ups, and interest messages.
- Keep messages brief, human, and specific.
- Prioritize channels that generate replies over channels that only feel clever.
- Follow up after several days if there is no reply.

## Interview Preparation

- Prepare before the first invite arrives.
- Prepare a concise `About me` / `Tell me about yourself` answer.
- Explain who you are, what you do well, what results you create, and why the role fits.
- Prepare your reason for leaving, target company profile, and compensation expectation.
- Practice technical answers in English.
- Prepare technical fundamentals, easy coding tasks, and STAR stories.
- Include a little personality so you sound like a future colleague, not only a skill list.

## Interview Tactics

- Use lower-priority companies to warm up.
- On HR screens, be clear, upbeat, and easy to talk to.
- Never trash previous employers.
- On technical rounds, answer directly and then expand with real examples.
- If you do not know something, reason out loud instead of freezing.
- Mirror the interviewer’s level of openness and communication style.
- Ask relevant questions in every round.
- After each round, log weak spots and fix them before the next one.
- Follow up if feedback stalls.
- Record interviews for review when possible.

## Negotiation

- Always ask about the compensation range.
- Always negotiate.
- Negotiate in writing where possible.
- Use competing processes or strong interview performance as leverage.
- Ask for improvement even if the first offer feels acceptable.
- Treat bonuses, options, tokens, and future raises as uncertain until realized.

## Legal, Tax, and Money

- Do not spend time on legal, tax, banking, relocation, or incorporation setup before you have a concrete offer.
- Treat contracts as operational, not protective.
- Assume a contract role can end quickly and keep a buffer.
- Prefer payment paths you can document.
- Keep invoices, contracts, statements, and supporting files.
- Once a concrete offer exists, verify the exact cross-border setup with a tax specialist for your jurisdiction.
- Optimize for something stable, repeatable, and explainable, not clever.

## Routine

- Every day: apply and maintain momentum.
- Every day: improve one weak point in the resume, profile, outreach, or interview prep.
- Every week: apply in batches if needed and review reply patterns.
- Every week: do direct outreach.
- Every week: practice technical and behavioral answers.
- Every week: publish a few LinkedIn posts.
- Every week: review interview notes and close gaps.
- Every month: take 1-2 interviews even if employed.
- Every month: recalibrate market rate from real conversations.
- Keep the process running for at least 1-3 months before judging it.

## Common Traps

- Waiting for perfect English.
- Overstudying instead of applying.
- Over-optimizing ATS details before getting market feedback.
- Obsessing over algorithms too early.
- Looking for secret channels instead of fixing the basics.
- Spending hours on paperwork before any offer exists.
- Expecting immediate results after a few applications.
